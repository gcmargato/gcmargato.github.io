Internationalism, elites and social reproduction: notes on the 80th anniversary of the United Nations

October 2025

We arrived late, due to our Bolt driver’s mistake, despite not being our common practice. When we arrived at High Institute for Social and Political Sciences’ building (ISCSP), the Foreign Affairs Minister’s delegation was also entering – as a consequence, we had to follow the delegation. Our entrance in the auditorium – called Adriano Moreira, it could not be otherwise – was, at least, anyhow, during the protocolar stop for the FAM’s official photo. I was there for an event co-organized by the Portuguese Diplomatic Institute, the ISCSP and the United Nations’ Regional Information Centre for Western Europe.

The auditorium was bigger than I expected, with more than 300 seats. I was trying to look for available seats for me and my friend – I found two of them near to the stage. I ended up seating in the first line after the three reserved for important guests. The seat was aside from two young men in suits and ties, wearing Sustainable Development Goals pins on their lapels. If my alterity was not defined yet, when I asked to pass to the wall side, as well as my posture and the way as I was dressed produced immediately a notable difference.

It has now been six years since I entered this urban world of International Relations, and I have found it difficult to get used to these events, which are academic in nature but bring together some of the country’s leading experts in International Relations alongside high-ranking political and diplomatic figures. These are spaces where a quasi-diplomatic habitus is practised – something the programme encouraged, setting aside time for networking during the coffee break – and where mimicking the elite serves the purpose of integrating into that very same elite – Bourdieu’s symbolic capital fits here perfectly. Of course, those born into the social and economic elites, particularly in Lisbon, are already halfway there, but they are not the only ones seeking this path. IR students are encouraged to participate in these events, ritualistic processes whose sole objective is socialisation within a normative sphere of ‘how things should be’ and ‘how one should behave’ – not necessarily for the diplomat, but for any human being who intends to work in IR – the adviser, the consultant, the manager, the lawyer, the professor, the television commentator, etc. – all of them must perform and be identifiable as part of an elite that has historically established itself as exclusive and which, therefore, seeks to remain as exclusive as possible. The entire social structure and the underlying assumptions of these events, which aspire to levels of formality rarely achieved, mirror the logic of the courts at the height of absolutism; participants attend primarily to be seen, intervene mainly to perform an ideal of intellectualism they have constructed for themselves, and spend the refreshments and breaks introducing themselves to diplomats, professors and bureaucrats because they realised early on that this would be the way to build a career in the field, under the banner of the aforementioned networking.

The formal atmosphere, the demand for conformity and integration, means that observation gives rise to the embodiment of a bourgeois ideal of order, which is mediated by the desirability of the participants’ behaviour – they must know how to present themselves, they must not laugh uncontrollably, they must be pleasant in their manner and speech, they must not intervene, they must only move under certain circumstances – despite the constant manifestation of a Queirozian logic, where protocol becomes impossible for the 300 people in a room to follow, and a possible approach is attempted, which is destabilised by disruptions instigated by political representatives who rarely attend the entire programme of the event, limiting themselves to an initial intervention, which, exceeding all possible limits for that very intervention, sees them immediately leave the venue, as if fleeing the plague, with protocol requiring the highest-ranking officials present to accompany them on their departure, before returning to their designated seats.

But this urgent need for order and social peace is also reflected in the thematic agenda of these events. The topics and speakers are chosen, above all, for their ability to foster consensus, thereby minimising the potential for disagreement and, as such, avoiding friction or the even more serious disruption to the schedule. The timetables are designed to minimise public participation, further reducing this potential for dissent. As these events are aimed primarily at students, it is not education as a source of critical thinking that is promoted, but rather education as a means of social reproduction that is sought. After five or ten lectures of this nature, students are able to present and discuss these topics in ways intended to be hegemonic and conducive to social order. Thus, through clichés, the thematic repertoires become almost unquestionable truths for the majority of those who choose to ‘study International Relations’ in the various senses that this idea may take.

Once the first set of presentations is over, the long-awaited coffee break arrives – which, in truth, is the highlight for all of us there. As is typical of such moments, I end up spending far too much time on social pleasantries, another aspect that protocol demands of us. Hugs, handshakes, the occasional kiss, the usual small talk – ‘What have you been up to?’ – and the odd comment from those who seek me out because they know of my (considerable) critical distance from the official line on foreign policy. Once these lengthy greetings are over, I make my way towards the refreshment table to nibble on something. Around the table, a man in a white uniform is shooing away potential gate-crashers who want to help themselves to that feast, like vultures waiting for a decomposing body. I watch from a distance – he only approaches younger people who are not wearing a suit. Some admit to being freeloaders, others express their indignation, offering to show the messages on their mobile phones – the said worker apologises and carries on.

Before leaving, I watch the comings and goings from the upper floor. I am not the only one not returning for the second part: the absence of the President of the Republic – who has excused himself on account of the death of Francisco Pinto Balsemão – means that a large part of the elite entourage, who had only come for the royal hand-kissing ceremony, leave for good, having already fulfilled their duty.
