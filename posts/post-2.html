Why memory still matters in foreign policy [AI testing]

April 2026 · 5 min read

When we think about foreign policy, we usually imagine institutions, strategic interests, alliances, and diplomatic calculations. Yet states also act through inherited narratives about themselves, their past, and their place in the world. Memory does not simply belong to the domestic sphere; it often helps structure how political communities define external threats, responsibilities, and ambitions.

This is one of the reasons why the study of memory matters for International Relations. Public debates about empire, dictatorship, revolution, or democratic transition are not only disputes about the past. They are also disputes about legitimacy in the present. In that sense, memory can shape foreign policy indirectly, by stabilising some political vocabularies while making others harder to articulate.

Memory as political orientation

Collective memory is often treated as symbolic or commemorative, but it also has practical effects. Political actors mobilise familiar historical images in order to describe the international environment, identify moral lessons, and justify particular courses of action. A country that narrates itself as peaceful, exceptional, victimised, anti-colonial, or civilisationally hybrid will not speak about the world in politically innocent ways.

These narratives do not determine policy in any automatic sense. Rather, they create repertoires of plausibility. They shape what sounds reasonable, responsible, or nationally coherent in a given moment. This is especially important in cases where official discourse depends on selective readings of difficult pasts.

The Portuguese case

In the Portuguese case, questions of empire, decolonisation, democracy, and Europe remain deeply entangled. References to lusophony, the democratic transition, the revolutionary process, or the so-called European option are not just historical markers. They continue to inform the language through which Portugal imagines its international role.

What interests me is not simply whether those narratives are historically accurate, but how they continue to work politically. Which pasts are remembered as usable? Which are displaced, softened, or depoliticised? And what kinds of foreign policy posture become easier to defend when some memories are made central and others peripheral?

From commemoration to international politics

A useful way to approach this problem is to stop treating commemoration as something separate from “real politics”. Museums, anniversaries, speeches, exhibitions, school narratives, and public controversies all contribute to the production of political common sense. They help define not only what a polity remembers, but also how it learns to speak about itself in the world.

This does not mean that memory explains everything. Material constraints, institutional settings, geopolitical pressures, and strategic calculations remain crucial. But a more complete account of foreign policy should also examine the symbolic and historical vocabularies that make some positions appear natural and others unthinkable.

A working hypothesis

My current working hypothesis is that mnemonic disputes are not secondary to political order; they are one of its conditions. If political communities seek stability not only in material security but also in continuity of self-understanding, then struggles over memory are also struggles over ontological security. This may help explain why apparently symbolic conflicts about monuments, textbooks, and commemorations can become politically intense.

For that reason, studying memory is not a detour away from International Relations. It is one way of seeing more clearly how international and domestic orders remain connected through discourse, identity, and historical imagination.

This is, at least, the question I want to keep exploring: not only what states do, but also what stories they need to tell in order to keep doing it.
